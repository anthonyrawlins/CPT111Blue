Not a local student?

RMIT considers you a local student if you are:

  • a citizen or permanent resident of Australia, or
  • a New Zealand citizen, or
  • a Temporary Protection visa (TPV) holder.

If you are unsure or hold a different visa type, please contact Info Corner for more information.

You’re viewing program information for international students.

Not an international student?

RMIT considers you an international student if you are:

  • intending to study on a student visa, or
  • not a citizen or permanent resident of Australia, or
  • not a New Zealand citizen, or
  • not a Temporary Protection visa (TPV) holder.

If you are unsure or hold a different visa type, please contact Info Corner for more information.

    24 Aug 2012

    RMIT's third year Environmental Science students head to Lakes Entrance for a week, conducting tests and taking their specimens into the lab at Bullock Island.

    Expand all sections

    Transcript

    TEXT ON SCREEN: Testing the waters. RMIT’s Environmental Science students undertake field studies at Lakes Entrance in south-east Victoria.

    VISUAL: Sally Harrison stands on a jetty in front of the Gippsland lakes. She talks to camera. A caption reads: “Sally Harrison PhD student and Advisor, RMIT University.”

    SALLY SPEAKS: So we’re here at Lakes Entrance with RMIT’s environmental science students. There’s about sixty students here with us for a week, the students go on a boat tour where they do sampling, they go up the river, and they also go on a fossil finding tour, bring back their samples and other specimens to the lab for analysis.

    VISUAL: Bradley Clarke stands on the waterfront. A caption reads: “Bradley Clarke, Research Associate, RMIT University.”

    BRADLEY SPEAKS: So we’ve brought students out into lake king, which is outside of Lakes Entrance. We’re taking water samples from different depths in the lake and then we’re also grabbing sediments samples to look for organisms that are there and also we do a plankton trall.

    VISUAL: Jeff Shimeta sits inside a laboratory and talks to camera. A caption reads: “Jeff Shimeta, Senior Lecturer, School of Applied Sciences.”

    JEFF SPEAKS: So we take them out to Lakes Entrance to apply skills in measuring the water quality and measuring biological parameters in the system, making collections and identifying organisms and measuring environmental impacts in the area. Lakes Entrance is a great place to do that as well because it has so many different environments.

    VISUAL: Scott Breschkin sitting outside, dressed in a lab coat, talking toward the camera. A caption reads: “Scott Breschkin, 3rd year Environmental Science student.”

    SCOTT SPEAKS: It was really good to be out in the field everyday doing the sampling, then also we had the opportunity then to go straight into the facilities down at lakes entrance where we had really good labs where the next day we could go through and look at all the samples collected and identify specimens and do further testing so that was a really good element to the trip.

    VISUAL: Rachael Boysen, talking toward the camera in the lab. A caption reads: “Rachael Boysen, 3rd Year Environmental Science Student, RMIT Univeristy”.

    RACHAEL SPEAKS: A really important part of the tip is basically learning how to use all the equipment, there’s so many different things but i think that was really good that you actually get to go out and have a hands on experience and see it rather than someone saying this piece of equipment does this, well that’s good, but when you can actually hold it, use it and see the results it’s giving, it can really help you understand.

    VISUAL: Jeff speaking, intercut with students working in the field.

    JEFF SPEAKS: Students learn a variety of subjects while they are here from different disciplines from chemistry and biology and physics. Everybody has their own speciality and so environmental science is often done as work by a team

    VISUAL: Rachael speaking, intercut with students working in the field.

    RACHAEL SPEAKS: Working in a large group, one of the most difficult aspects is the communication between everybody, as soon as you lose communication, it doesn’t work, so it really forced us to effectively communicate with each other. Over the duration of the research you learn some people are more suited to different aspects than others.

    VISUAL: Jeff speaking, intercut with students working in the field.

    JEFF SPEAKS: Well we’ve modelled a lot of these exercise after monitoring techniques that are used by State government agencies and consulting firms to monitor the environment. So the team building exercises and the teamwork that they do are directly applicable to the working environment that they will be in eventually.

    VISUAL: Scott speaking to camera.

    SCOTT SPEAKS: Yeah so I think some of the skills and methodology that we used down in Lakes Entrance were really helpful for me to show that i’d done these kinds of things when i went for a job interview and it really helped me to secure a graduate position with a consultancy next year.

    VISUAL: fast forward time lapse images of students working in a laboratory.

    [END OF TRANSCRIPT]
