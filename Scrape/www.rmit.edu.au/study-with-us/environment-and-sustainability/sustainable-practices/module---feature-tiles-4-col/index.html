Not a local student?

RMIT considers you a local student if you are:

  • a citizen or permanent resident of Australia, or
  • a New Zealand citizen, or
  • a Temporary Protection visa (TPV) holder.

If you are unsure or hold a different visa type, please contact Info Corner for more information.

You’re viewing program information for international students.

Not an international student?

RMIT considers you an international student if you are:

  • intending to study on a student visa, or
  • not a citizen or permanent resident of Australia, or
  • not a New Zealand citizen, or
  • not a Temporary Protection visa (TPV) holder.

If you are unsure or hold a different visa type, please contact Info Corner for more information.

With a passion for understanding the engineering process of products from start to finish, Daniel Chan is studying an undergraduate degree in sustainable systems engineering.

Job title and organisation:
RMIT program:

Bachelor of Engineering (Sustainable Systems Engineering) (Honours)
Scholarship or opportunity:
Research field:
Country of origin:
Destination:

I chose to study sustainable systems engineering because I wanted to learn about the engineering process of products from start to finish.

I chose to study sustainable systems engineering because the degree takes into account all aspects of the production process, from the initial design and brainstorming phase, all the way through research and development and the final stage of product delivery.

I’m interested in the whole life cycle of a product, including optimising efficiencies and identifying areas of improvement. Increasing efficiencies in systems will ultimately increase performance and be greatly beneficial in the future.

We’ve covered subjects from electrical and mechanical to chemical engineering, broadening my knowledge and enhancing my understanding of the overall engineering process. I’ve really enjoyed learning about the broad spectrum of engineering.

I’m really interested in the automotive industry and hope to work as a senior systems engineer in this area.

Transcript

The Master of Engineering (Sustainable Energy) teaches the technologies and practices needed to lead the future of sustainable energy, including: energy efficiency, renewable power generation systems such as solar, wind, hydro, wave and tidal. Energy storage technologies such as hydrogen systems and batteries, thermal systems such as solar thermal collectors, geothermal, cogeneration and trigeneration systems, biomass and solar fuels and social, environmental and economic context for sustainable energy.

This program provides a pathway for engineers and scientists, and those with an alternative acceptable qualification and significant industry experience.

This video features several people talking to camera interspersed with footage of students working on sustainable energy projects. Upbeat music plays throughout.

Duration: 2:24 minutes

[Opening title]
Master of Engineering (Sustainable Energy)
School of Computer Science and IT
RMIT staff and students talk about their motivations and what it’s like to study sustainable energy.

Asma Mohamad Aris [Master of Engineering (Sustainable Energy) RMIT University]: I want something that is fun, something that is new, but also beneficial to my future.

Darcy Denton [Master of Engineering (Sustainable Energy) RMIT University]: I guess I like the field of sustainable energy in engineering because it allows me to use my skills in maths and science, whilst also creating some good.

Srirag Kerachan [Master of Engineering (Sustainable Energy) RMIT University]: I have done my Bachelor's in electronics instrumentation, so I was looking at the starting phase for a PhD or getting into a renewable energy sector.

Asma Mohamad Aris: Sustainable energy is not just saving the environment or learning about the environmental technology. It's more than that. It's like putting equations of social responsibility, ethics, even economic point of view, and not to forget the engineering aspect of the technology.

Dr. Shabani [School of Aerospace, Mech. And Manufacturing Engineering]:  The program places the students in a very competitive position to grab international and local jobs. The strategy of this program in particular is just training work-ready students.

Asma Mohamad Aris: I've got to learn like new technologies, new alternative energies. I believe in sustainable life and climate change, so sustainable energies sort of like bring all of these components under one roof.

Dr Bahman Shabani: As part of the program, the students should do a project, a research project , and they’ve got the opportunity to go for whatever area that they're interested in.

Asma Mohamad Aris: My final year project, I did it with Professor Gary Rosengarten in designing and simulating the solar thermal collector systems for low energy residential building.

Srirag Kerachan: Most of our programs, we have a group project and single project, so when we interact with these people, you sit down, you learn. It's more of an interaction.

Darcy Denton: We have a number of guest lecturers who come in and talk to us from the industries. It's great to see what people are doing in the real world, and where the actual jobs are.

Dr Bahman Shabani: Climate change is not a hoax. It's happening now around the world. The demand for professionals working in this area is just growing and growing.

Darcy Denton: There's a perception that somewhere in the future, we're going to reach a point where renewable energy's viable. But I think we've already got to that point in a number of cases with the promise that renewables have world-wide and into the future. This is a field that people should be moving into. It's great to get on board.
